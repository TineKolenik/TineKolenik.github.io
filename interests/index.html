Detailed Research Interests

As a cognitive scientist, the profession affords me to study or at least maintain an informed interest in a large number of research areas, but closely following the model of a “T-shaped individual”. This really suits me as an interdisciplinarian who is drawn to a a significant number of different subjects.

Paradigmatically, I am especially interested in 4E cognition (and, by extension, the free energy principle and predictive coding) with emphasis on enactivism, which represents the synthesis of cognitive science, evolutionary biology (autopoiesis, autonomy, adaptivity), (complex) systems theory, (neuro)phenomenology, and radical constructivism & second-order cybernetics. I am particularly fond of the sensorimotor approaches to cognition and the life-mind continuity thesis (the idea that where there is life, there is mind; that what is alive is also cognitive). Currently, I am specifically interested in enactivism in connection to minimal cognition, how to apply enactivism to empirical research and sociocultural phenomena, and how to investigate enactive properties computationally.

Methodologically, I am especially interested in computer modelling & simulation (including artificial intelligence, artificial life and artificial societies). Philosophically and theoretically, I am particularly interested in what the extent and reach of computational methods to model cognitive agents is, how cognitive science can inform increasingly big data-driven computational approaches to mimic, predict and understand cognition, and how to relate computational models to reality when living organisms are modelled. Practically, I am particularly interested in cognitive & sociological modelling & simulation. Currently, I am specifically interested to what extent cognition and related phenomena computational approaches can elucidate as well as computational epistemology more widely, and in the genetic algorithm and its fitness function – how to characterize it, how it relates to the artificial organisms and their environment, how to make it dynamic and co-determinable by artificial organisms’ inner workings, etc. I am starting to investigate the ways of bridging the gap between purely computational and cognitive architectures in various artificial agents that mimic intelligent behavior as well as how to set up computational platforms for comparing different ideas and theories about cognition.

Another topic that I am investigating is whether and how inner mechanisms can be deduced from behavior and vice versa. The big question is what the fact that computational models can (almost perfectly) mimic or predict behavior of living beings means for our understanding of these living beings, if anything.

My research is thoroughly interweaved with philosophy of science, philosophy of cognitive science and philosophy of mind. One of my foremost interests is how presuppositions (e.g., epistemological), biases (e.g., racial; in various disciplines, e.g., in machine learning) and intuitions (e.g., folk psychology), be it manifested through researchers’ behavior (e.g., demand characteristics), affect research, from design to outcome. This is why understanding many problems of (proper) research design (e.g., ecological validity) is key to my endeavors.

Areas that I also study and investigate include:

  • sociocultural role in cognition (e.g., the relation between social and biological determinism)
  • evolution (of cognition and perception)
  • biophilosophy, philosophy of evolution
  • group cognition & collective intelligence (especially in terms of emergent properties, but also applications like human computation)
  • theories of communication
  • the role of behavioral sciences in policy-making and governing
  • folk science and psychology
  • cognitive ethology
  • cognitive ergonomics

I closely follow the practices and am avidly in favor of open science. I try to contribute to as much quality scientific communication as possible.
